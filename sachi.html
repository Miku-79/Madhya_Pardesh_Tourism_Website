Welcome to Sanchi

Discover the ancient Buddhist marvel

UNESCO World Heritage Site

The Great Stupa at Sanchi is one of India's most remarkable Buddhist monuments, dating back to the 3rd century BCE. Originally commissioned by Emperor Ashoka to house the relics of Buddha, this magnificent structure stands as a testament to the rich Buddhist heritage of ancient India. Over centuries, the stupa was expanded and embellished by subsequent rulers, making it an extraordinary chronicle of the evolution of Buddhist art and architecture from its early beginnings to its mature form.

Historical Significance

Sanchi's significance lies in its unbroken development from the 3rd century BCE to the 12th century CE. It was established by Emperor Ashoka of the Mauryan dynasty who built the original stupa after converting to Buddhism. The site was further developed during the Sunga, Satavahana, and Gupta periods, each adding distinctive architectural elements. Despite the decline of Buddhism in India, Sanchi remained untouched and was rediscovered in 1818 by British officer General Taylor.

Architectural Marvel

The Great Stupa (Stupa No. 1) is the most impressive structure at Sanchi, with a height of 16.5 meters and a diameter of 36.6 meters. Its hemispherical dome represents Buddha's cosmos, and it features four elaborately carved toranas (ornamental gateways) facing the four cardinal directions. These gateways are adorned with intricate sculptures depicting tales from Buddha's life, Jataka stories, and scenes of everyday life, providing valuable insights into ancient Indian civilization.

Buddhist Monuments

Besides the Great Stupa, the Sanchi complex houses numerous other structures, including smaller stupas, monasteries, temples, and pillars. Stupa No. 2 is known for its stone balustrade and Stupa No. 3 reportedly contained relics of important Buddhist disciples Sariputta and Mahamogallana. The Ashoka Pillar, with its famous capital of four lions (now adopted as India's national emblem), once stood near the southern gateway of the Great Stupa.

Sculptural Excellence

The sculptural art at Sanchi represents the zenith of ancient Indian craftsmanship. The toranas display relief carvings that narrate Buddha's life events without depicting him in human form (following the early Buddhist aniconic tradition). Instead, symbols like the Bodhi tree, footprints, and the wheel of dharma represent the Buddha. The sculptures also portray urban scenes, royal processions, battles, and celebrations, offering a vivid glimpse into life in ancient India.
